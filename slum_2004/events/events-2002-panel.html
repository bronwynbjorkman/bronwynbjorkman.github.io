Panel on Language Planning in Québec March 12, 2003
Martin Auditorium, McIntyre Medical Building

Since its adoption and implementation in 1977 intended to protect and affirm French as the official language of Quebec, the Charte de la langue française has been, and still remains the target of much debate and discussion. But are we debating the same issues that we were twenty-five years ago? Perhaps not.

•What impact does the undeniable need for the mastery of English have upon French in Quebec?
•How can we prepare our children for the reality of English's near-universal use, nurturing French as a native language while also bringing up our children to be "citizens of the world?"
•Could bilingual education be considered as a means to give Quebecers the tools to play a more important role in the international community?

The panel discussion was organised by Society of Linguistics Undergraduates of McGill and the Political Science Students' Association, and was followed by a French-English bilingual question period and an informal reception where discussions continued.

Panelists:

Nadia Brédimas-Assimopoulos, President, Conseil supérieur de la langue française, Government of Québec
The Conseil supérieur de la langue française is one of the organisations created by the Charte de la langue française. Its role is to consult with the minister responsible for the application of current laws on any question pertaining to the French language in Québec. In doing so, the Conseil advises the minister on any topic the latter may present it with and brings forward issues that it considers require the government's attention.

Mela Sarkar, Associate Professor, Faculty of Education, McGill University
Professor Sarkar is an associate professor in the department of integrated studies at McGill University. Currently, she is focussing on the acquisition of French (as a first and as a second language) by both child and adult minority-language speakers in Montreal classrooms. Her work draws on the classroom-centered second language acquisition research tradition and has implications for educational planning and policy decisions in Quebec, especially for French second language curriculum design and teacher training. Having studied and worked in Quebec, she has acquired knowledge regarding educational policies in Quebec.

Denise Daoust, Professor Emeritus, Linguistics, UQAM
Professor Daoust's fields of study include language planning and the sociology of language (French and English). She has specifically looked at language in the workplace, examining sociological, communicational and psychosocial factors such as age, attitude and perception, regarding both languages as well as francisation. Among her numerous publications are "The Evaluation of Sociolinguistic and Terminological Change in a Commercial Enterprise" and "A Decade of Language Planning in Québec".

Alan Patten, Associate Professor, Political Science, McGill University
Professor Patten's interests lie in contemporary, Anglo-American political theory and the history of political thought. Previously, he has done research on Hegel's political philosophy, as well as the relationship between liberalism and nationalism. More specifically, Professor Patten has written several papers pertaining to issues such as secession and self-determination, the basis for cultural rights in liberal egalitarian though, and the connection between citizenship and national identity. Currently, he is writing a book on language politics and normative political theory.
